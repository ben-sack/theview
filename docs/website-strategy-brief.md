# The View — Website Strategy Brief

---

## 1. Brand Translation for Web

The website is not a marketing tool — it's an artifact of the brand. It should feel like something you *found*, not something that found you.

**Tone:** Understated confidence. The copy should never sell; it should describe. Short sentences. No hype language. Let negative space do work that words would cheapen.

**Visual language:** Warm, analog-adjacent, restrained. Typography carries more weight than photography. If photography is used, it should feel like someone's rolls — not a venue shoot. No stock. No neon. No gradient overlays. Think late evening in a well-appointed Westside house: warm neutrals, deep tones, soft light.

**Mystery as a feature:** The site should not explain everything. Leaving deliberate gaps creates curiosity and signals that the full experience lives *off* the website.

---

## 2. Audience Assumptions

- **Age:** 27–42, skewing creative professional
- **Geography:** Westside LA and surrounding creative pockets (Culver City, Silver Lake, Echo Park, Venice, West Adams)
- **Background:** Music industry, film, design, fashion, architecture, tech-adjacent
- **Mindset:** Discovery-oriented. They value knowing about something before it's known. They disengage immediately from anything that feels try-hard, over-produced, or mass-market.
- **Arrival path:** Almost certainly word-of-mouth, a shared link, or a story tap — not a Google search
- **Device:** Mobile-first, but not exclusively mobile

---

## 3. Website Goals

1. Signal that this is *real* and *intentional* — not a concept, not a pop-up hustle
2. Communicate the ethos without over-explaining it
3. Create a mechanism for genuine interest to surface — one that preserves the gate
4. Serve as a reference point for existing community members (event cadence, context)
5. Be inherently share-worthy — something a member would send to someone they're vouching for

---

## 4. Conversion Goals

Traditional conversion metrics don't apply here. The gate *is* the product.

- **Primary:** Request for consideration / expression of interest (not a newsletter signup — an *application*)
- **Secondary:** Social follow (Instagram, possibly Substack for a more editorial angle)
- **Tertiary:** Word-of-mouth amplification — the site should feel worth sending to exactly one specific person

The CTA framing matters enormously. "Sign up" is wrong. "Apply" is close but clinical. Something like *"Get in touch"* or *"Express interest"* — or no label at all, just a context-setting line before the form — will land better.

---

## 5. Recommended Page Structure

**Single long-scroll, section-anchored** is the right architecture. Multi-page nav implies more content than this brand should show. Everything should be accessible in one continuous experience.

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Opening / Hero** | One image or texture, the name, a single orienting line — nothing more |
| **The Concept** | 3–5 sentences. What this is. Not what it isn't. |
| **Music** | A curated Spotify playlist, a brief statement, or a rotating artist spotlight. This is the soul of the brand — give it room. |
| **Archive** | Sparse visual record of past gatherings. Establishes history and realness. No captions needed. |
| **Express Interest** | Minimal form. Name, email, one open question. No checkboxes. No dropdowns. |

---

## 6. Key Risks to Avoid

**Over-explaining.** If the site has to say "exclusive," it isn't. Let behavior and restraint communicate that.

**Generic nightlife aesthetics.** Dark background + glowing serif + ambient music = every club website from 2019. The View needs to look nothing like that.

**Corporate copy.** *"We bring people together through the power of music"* is the death of this brand on-page. Every line of copy should pass the test: would a real person in this community say this out loud?

**Friction mismatch.** Too easy to request an invite cheapens the concept. Too hard or too cold loses people who are genuinely right for it. The form should feel like a conversation starter, not a bouncer.

**Overpromising through design.** A site that's more polished than the actual experience creates a gap that erodes trust. The site should feel slightly more intimate than impressive.

**Treating mobile as an afterthought.** The primary user is on their phone, sent here by someone they trust. The mobile experience is the experience.

---

The through-line for every design and copy decision: *would someone who belongs here feel like they found something, or like they were marketed to?*
